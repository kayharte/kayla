How can we use archaeological data to inform our future in Climate Change?

Archaeology, generally thought of as digging up the past, is now being used to analyse past climate change with tree rings, ice cores, fossilized biological data. Questions of how plant and animal species adapted to these changes, how they migrated and how ecosystems changed in one geographical place are being answered.

In a climate that seems to be changing more quickly than ever, how can we create models for change to ease into a changing future?

Fossilized pollen samples are appearing to be the most informative by discovering drought/flood cycles and deviations from weather patterns even later than the holocene. All over the world, and in a large variety of sites, archaeologists are researching applications of fossilized pollen for a future climate change.

Reflections

Portfolio I

I was interested in addressing climate issues of today in an archaeological way. To do this, I hope to be able to connect what we know of past climates to create models for adaptation. While physical scientists have done so, archaeologists need to get in the game of climate change which I hope to do (on a small scale) on this site.

I chose an earthy vibe for this single paged blog using my own photos taken at Kennedy Lake near Clayoqout Sound, the site of the largest civil disobedience in Canada in the 90's 'War in the Woods', which successfully prevented logging of the temperate rainforest in the area. It left behind remnants of a logging bridge, as pictured, and as an archaeological symbol to what occurred in this place. This is a recent example, but civilizations have been adapting to changing climates and the need to survive within them for millennia. From these past experiences of climate change, we can learn how adaptation occurred, and, with the incorporation of new technologies, mitigate the future.

The single page set up of this website might prove to be counterproductive when adding more information to the site as I continue through the course and looking back, maybe should have chosen a more complex layout. I found html easily changeable but have has many struggles with css and what I have altered from the template took a significant amount of time to do. The title is meant to emphasize that archaeology isn't just the study of 'old things and dead things' but has applications for the future! While the connection between the images and the titles may be obvious to me, I'm not sure that they are intuitive to the average reader/viewer and may need to adapt this in the future as I add things to the site. In addition, the way the template was laid out was monochromatically so I had issues adapting it to a different colour scheme.
